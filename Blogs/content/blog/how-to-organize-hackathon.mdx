# How to Organize a Hackathon: Complete Step-by-Step Guide

You've decided to run a hackathon. Now what?

This guide walks you through every phase: planning, registration, submission, judging, and announcement. Whether it's your first event or your tenth, this framework works.

## Phase 1: Planning (8-12 Weeks Before)

### Week 1-2: Define Your Vision
**Questions to answer:**
- **Format**: Online? In-person? Hybrid?
- **Duration**: 24-hour sprint, weekend event, multi-week challenge?
- **Audience**: Employees, students, external public?
- **Theme**: Open innovation or specific problem area (climate, healthcare, fintech)?
- **Size**: 20 people? 500? 5,000?
- **Budget**: $1,000? $50,000? Sponsored?

**Deliverable**: 1-page event brief (problem statement, target audience, expected outcomes)

### Week 3-4: Logistics Planning
**Decisions:**
- **Date & time**: What works for your audience?
- **Venue** (if in-person): Space, capacity, parking, accessibility
- **Technology**: Which platform for registration, submission, judging?
- **Judges**: Who will evaluate? How many?
- **Prizes**: What's the incentive? ($$$, jobs, mentorship, recognition?)
- **Marketing**: How will you recruit participants?

**Action items:**
- Book venue
- Reserve platform
- Identify judge pool

### Week 5-6: Budget & Sponsorship
**Allocate budget for:**
- Platform/software: $200–$2,000
- Venue rental: $0–$5,000
- Prizes: $0–$10,000+
- Catering (if in-person): $0–$3,000
- Marketing/promotion: $500–$2,000
- Staff time: not budgeted but track it

**Find sponsors** to offset costs (tech companies, nonprofits, government agencies).

### Week 7-8: Build Your Judging Criteria
**Define:**
- **What are judges evaluating?** (innovation, feasibility, impact, design, business model?)
- **Scoring scale**: 1-5? 1-10? Rubric-based?
- **Weights**: Is innovation worth more than feasibility?
- **Multi-round format**: Screening, deep-dive, deliberation?

**Deliverable**: Judging rubric (shared publicly so participants understand what matters)

### Week 9-10: Marketing & Recruitment
**Start spreading the word:**
- Social media posts (weekly through event)
- Email to target communities
- Blogs, communities, universities
- Direct outreach to potential participants
- Create landing page with event details

**Goal**: Build awareness 4-6 weeks before launch

### Week 11-12: Set Up Infrastructure
**Platform configuration:**
- Registration form (customize fields)
- Event page with details, rules, judging criteria
- Submission form (what files/info will projects include?)
- Judge dashboard setup
- Email templates for notifications

**Testing**: Dry-run the entire workflow yourself (register, submit, judge, see results)

## Phase 2: Registration (6 Weeks Before - 2 Weeks Before)

### Open Registration
**Timeline**: 4-6 weeks before hackathon
**Setup:**
- Share registration link
- Answer FAQs (What is a team? Can I participate solo? How many per team?)
- Track registration numbers
- Send reminder emails (2 weeks out, 1 week out)

**Typical conversion**: 20-30% of registrations actually show up/submit

### Team Formation (if applicable)
- For in-person: registration might include team members or team-finding process
- For online: let teams form independently; they'll find each other on Discord/Slack

### Pre-Event Communication
- Send email: "Event starts Friday; here's the link"
- Share: Final judging criteria, rules, submission format
- Post: FAQ document, judge bios, prize details

## Phase 3: Hackathon Event (The Actual Days)

### Day 1: Kickoff
**Timeline**: ~2 hours
- **Welcome & context**: Why are we doing this? What problems are we solving?
- **Judge introductions**: Meet the judges; understand their backgrounds
- **Submission walkthrough**: Show exactly how to submit a project
- **Networking**: Let teams form or get to know each other
- **Breakout help sessions**: Q&A on technical setup, brainstorming

**Output**: Teams formed, clear on the challenge, excited energy

### Days 2-3: Building & Coding Sprint
- Participants are heads-down coding/building
- You're available for questions (Slack channel, office hours)
- Share: Encouraging messages, progress updates
- Track: Any missing/struggling teams?

### Final Day: Wrap-Up & Submission Deadline
- Announce: "Submissions close in 6 hours"
- Final reminder: 2 hours before deadline
- Close submissions at deadline (no extensions)
- Notify judges: "Projects are ready for review"

**Pro tip**: Expect 80% of submissions in the final 2 hours. This is normal.

## Phase 4: Judging (1-2 Weeks After Event)

### Pre-Judging: Set Judge Expectations
- Email judges: "You're assigned X projects. Please evaluate by Y date."
- Share rubric and evaluation instructions
- Share what each project looks like (demo video, GitHub link, description)

### Execution: Multi-Round Judging

**If using platform:**
1. Round 1: All judges quickly screen all projects (5-10 min each)
2. Intermediate: Platform auto-calculates; top 50% advance
3. Round 2: Specialty judges deep-evaluate finalists (30-45 min each)
4. Final: Leadership panel discusses and selects winners

**If manual:**
1. Send judges all projects
2. Ask for scores by deadline
3. Collect feedback
4. Manually consolidate scores
5. Leadership team discusses any ties/anomalies

**Timeline**: Don't let this drag. 1-2 weeks max.

## Phase 5: Winner Announcement
plan:
- **Announce publicly**: Press release, social media, email
- **Feature winners**: Blog posts, interviews, showcase projects
- **Award ceremony**: If in-person, celebrate with photos
- **Video content**: Document judges discussing winning projects

**Make winners feel celebrated.** This encourages repeat participation and word-of-mouth.

## Phase 6: Post-Hackathon

### Feedback to Participants
- Share results: leaderboard, winner announcements
- Provide feedback to top non-winning projects ("Keep iterating, you're close")
- Share judge commentary anonymized ("Judges loved your idea but code needs work")

### Feedback to Judges
- Thank you email
- Debrief: "Which projects surprised you?"
- Retrospective: "How can we improve the process next year?"

### Post-Event Reporting
- Document attendance, project count, judge feedback
- Calculate ROI: cost per participant, quality of ideas
- Create retrospective: what worked, what to change next year?

### Long-Term Follow-Up
- Stay in touch with winning teams (offer mentorship, next steps)
- Invite new judges from this year's participants (build community)
- Start planning next hackathon (typically annual)

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

**Mistake 1: Unclear judging criteria**
- Don't leave judges guessing what they're evaluating
- **Fix**: Publish rubric early; training call for judges

**Mistake 2: Compressed timeline**
- "Let's do the hackathon in a week" doesn't give time for judging
- **Fix**: Plan for 2-3 weeks end-to-end

**Mistake 3: No platform (everything via email)**
- Leads to chaos, lost submissions, misaligned expectations
- **Fix**: Use a platform designed for hackathons

**Mistake 4: Weak prizes**
- "$20 Amazon gift card" for winners doesn't motivate
- **Fix**: Meaningful prizes (cash, job offers, mentorship, recognition)

**Mistake 5: No communication post-judging**
- Participants wait weeks for results; lose momentum
- **Fix**: Announce within 1-2 weeks max

## Key Success Factors

1. **Clear rubric published early**: Participants know what judges care about
2. **Responsive communication**: Answer questions quickly; keep energy high
3. **Quality judges**: Credible, engaged evaluators improve trust
4. **Transparent results**: Leaderboards, feedback, winner announcements
5. **Celebrate winners**: Make-or-break moment for repeat participation and word-of-mouth

## Conclusion

A well-organized hackathon requires planning across 8-12 weeks and execution across 2-3 weeks. Use this framework:
- **Planning phase**: Set vision, logistics, judges, platform
- **Registration phase**: Market and recruit
- **Event phase**: Kickoff, building time, submissions
- **Judging phase**: Multi-round evaluation  
- **Announcement**: Celebrate winners
- **Post-event**: Feedback and retrospective

Follow this playbook and you'll run a professional, fair, and memorable hackathon that leaves participants wanting to participate again.

**Next step**: Pick your hackathon date. Work backwards using this timeline. You're ready to go.
