# How to Evaluate Startup Pitches Fairly: Complete Judging Framework

You're judging a startup pitch event. How do you score fairly across 20 pitches so the best ideas win, not just the most charismatic founders?

A structured framework beats gut feeling every time.

## The Five-Criterion Framework

### **1. Market Opportunity (30%)**
- How big is the addressable market?
- Is there real customer pain?
- How urgent is the problem?

**5/5**: $10B+ market, clear customer demand, urgency evident
**3/5**: $100M-$1B market, assumed demand, moderate urgency
**1/5**: $10M or less market, unclear need, "nice-to-have"

### **2. Team Capability (25%)**
- Do they have complementary skills?
- Track record of shipping?
- Founder-market fit (do they understand their space)?

**5/5**: Founder previously built successful company in domain; strong technical + business co-founders
**3/5**: One founder experienced; other is learning; team is complete
**1/5**: No relevant experience; founder recently learning domain

### **3. Product-Market Fit Likelihood (25%)**
- Do they have validation? (customers, letters of intent, MVP)
- Is their solution differentiated?
- Can they acquire customers?

**5/5**: 50+ customers, $100K ARR, clear unfair advantage
**3/5**: MVP with interest, some early customers, competing on execution
**1/5**: Idea only, no customer validation, undifferentiated approach

### **4. Execution Ability (15%)**
- Realistic roadmap for next 12 months?
- Do they understand the funding they'd need?
- Is the team's product ship velocity clear?

**5/5**: Clear milestones, realistic budget, team shipped multiple products
**3/5**: Reasonable road map, funding estimate, shipping experience
**1/5**: Vague plan, unrealistic timeline, first-time founders

### **5. Pitch Quality (5%)**
- Communication clear?
- Answering the right questions?
- Memorable?

(This matters least, but communication does affect investor perception)

## Scoring Example

**Pitch #1: "AI Copilot for Therapists"**
- Market: $3B annually (mental health tech booming) — **5/5**
- Team: CEO = psychologist + tech founder; CTO ex-ML engineer — **5/5**
- Product: 100 therapy practices using beta; $50K MRR — **5/5**
- Execution: Clear roadmap, raised $500K seed already, monthly growth 15% — **4/5**
- Pitch: Clear, compelling, answered all questions — **5/5**

**Weighted score:** (5×0.30) + (5×0.25) + (5×0.25) + (4×0.15) + (5×0.05) = 1.5 + 1.25 + 1.25 + 0.6 + 0.25 = **4.85/5** = Top tier

**Pitch #2: "Blockchain for Supply Chain"**
- Market: $2B (exists, but many competitors) — **3/5**
- Team: MBA grad, first-time founder, tech hire being recruited — **2/5**
- Product: Whitepaper only, no customer conversations, MVP in progress — **1/5**
- Execution: Roadmap exists, but ambitious given team size — **2/5**
- Pitch: Polished, but avoided hard questions about competition — **3/5**

**Weighted score:** (3×0.30) + (2×0.25) + (1×0.25) + (2×0.15) + (3×0.05) = 0.9 + 0.5 + 0.25 + 0.3 + 0.15 = **2.1/5** = Bottom tier

## Pre-Judging Checklist

**Before pitches start:**
1. All judges have rubric and understand criteria
2. Do a calibration session (watch 1 pitch together + discuss scores)
3. Ensure judges know: "We're scoring the opportunity + team, not the pitch"
4. Establish Q&A rules ("You get 3 questions per judge")

## During Judging

**Setup:**
- Judges have notebook + rubric checklist
- Q&A structured (each judge asks 1-2 questions)
- Audience quiet (focus on founders + judges)
- Record pitches (listen again later if unsure)

**Judge questions to ask:**
- "What's one thing that could make this fail?" (tests realism)
- "How will you find your first 100 customers?" (tests go-to-market)
- "Who's your biggest competitor? How are you different?" (tests competitive thinking)
- "If I gave you $500K today, how would you spend it?" (tests prioritization)

## Post-Pitch Scoring

**Immediately after each pitch:**
- Judges score individually (no discussion yet)
- Takes 2-3 minutes per pitch
- Keeps scores honest (not influenced by other judges)

**After all pitches:**
- Consolidate scores
- Identify outliers ("Why did Judge A score 5/5 and Judge B score 2/5?")
- Discuss top 5 and bottom 5
- Deliberate on close calls

## Avoiding Bias

**Anchoring bias**: First pitch is fresh, last pitch is tired
- **Fix**: Judges rate independently; no influence from previous pitches

**Similarity bias**: Favor founders who remind you of yourself
- **Fix**: Use rubric; focus on metrics, not "vibe fit"

**Charisma bias**: Polished speaker scores higher than awkward genius
- **Fix**: This is why pitch is only 5% weight. Judge the idea/team, not the presenter.

**Confirmation bias**: You like Pitch A, so you remember facts that support it
- **Fix**: Ask critical questions; push back on early favorites

## Timeline

**1 month before:**
- [ ] Invite judges; confirm attendance
- [ ] Build rubric (share with judges)
- [ ] Judges calibrate (watch sample pitch + discuss)

**1 week before:**
- [ ] Confirm pitch order
- [ ] Brief judges on logistics

**Pitch day:**
- [ ] Opening remarks (5 min)
- [ ] Pitches + Q&A (20 min × # of startups)
- [ ] Judges score (immediately after each pitch)
- [ ] Deliberation + result (leaders discuss top finalists)

**Post-event:**
- [ ] Announce winner
- [ ] Share feedback (optional but appreciated)

## Output: Leaderboard

After judging, publish:
```

2. Quantum Tech Solutions - 4.2/5 - Innovative tech, execution unclear
3. ...
```

Transparency builds credibility.

## Conclusion

Fair pitch evaluation requires:
- Clear criterion (Market, Team, Product, Execution, Pitch)
- Weighted scoring (market = most important)
- Independent judging (no influence from others)
- Structured Q&A (same questions for all)
- Rubric as safety rail (prevents gut-feeling biases)

**Before your next pitch event**: Build this rubric. Train judges. Watch pitch quality and credibility soar.
