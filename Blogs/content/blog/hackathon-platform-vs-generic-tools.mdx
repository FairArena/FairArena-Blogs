# Specialized Hackathon Platforms vs Airtable/Notion/Jira: Common Mistakes

Teams often try to run hackathons on generic tools they already have: Airtable for submissions, Jira for project tracking, Notion for documentation.

This always ends in frustration. Here's why—and what works better.

## The Airtable Trap

**Why people choose Airtable for hackathons:**
- "We already have it and know how to use it"
- "We can customize the database to track submissions"
- "It's flexible"

**What goes wrong:**

1. **No submission intake flow**: Participants see a bare database. They don't know what they're supposed to fill in. Context is missing.

2. **Judge experience is terrible**: Judges open Airtable and see a spreadsheet. They have to click through 200 rows to find their assigned projects. There's no rubric, no scoring interface, no progress tracking.

3. **No automation**: When a submission is "rejected," nobody tells the participant. When judging is complete, scores don't auto-calculate. You handle all workflows manually.

4. **Version chaos**: Judges download the Airtable sheet, score it locally, email it back. You now have 10 different versions of the scoring sheet. Which do you use?

5. **No audit trail**: You can't prove who judged what or trace how decisions were made.

6. **Duplicate work**: You end up with forms in Typeform (for submissions), spreadsheets in Airtable (for tracking), and judgments in email, then manually reconcile everything.

**Verdict**: Airtable is great for structured data. It's terrible for workflows where you need different interfaces for different roles (participant vs judge vs organizer).

## The Notion Problem

**Why people choose Notion:**
- "Everything goes in Notion"
- "People know how to use it"
- "We can create forms for submissions"

**What goes wrong:**

1. **Slow UI for scale**: Notion is great up to 100 items. At 500+ submissions, it bogs down.

2. **Submission forms are clunky**: Notion Forms work, but they're not optimized for detailed submissions. File uploads often fail. Embedding is limited.

3. **No structured judging**: There's no rubric builder, no scoring workflows, no judge assignment logic.

4. **Notification chaos**: Notion mentions are easy to miss. Judges won't see they're assigned a project. Participants won't know when decisions are made.

5. **Limited integration**: Getting data out of Notion is tedious. You can't easily connect it to leaderboards, email, or other tools.

6. **Not scalable for live events**: Can't handle real-time judge scoring for live hackathons.

**Verdict**: Notion is a knowledge base / personal wiki. It's not built for structured business workflows or large-scale event management.

## The Jira Misconception

**Why people choose Jira for hackathons:**
- "We use it for regular projects, so hackathons are just another project"
- "It tracks tasks and status"

**What goes wrong:**

1. **Wrong mental model**: Jira is for breaking big work into smaller tasks. Hackathons require collecting ideas and evaluating them. Different workflows entirely.

2. **Participant confusion**: Developers don't want to use Jira. They want a simple submission form. Forcing them into Jira creates friction.

3. **No judging workflow**: Jira can track tasks, but it has no concept of evaluation rubrics, blind review, or structured scoring.

4. **Overhead for small events**: Jira's power is wasted on hackathons. You're configuring boards, custom fields, workflows when you just need "submit → judge → announce."

5. **Poor for non-technical participants**: Jira has a learning curve. If your hackathon includes non-engineers (designers, product people, business people), Jira feels like overkill.

**Verdict**: Jira is for software development teams. It's not for managing competitions or running hackathons.

## Why These Generic Tools Fail

All three (Airtable, Notion, Jira) optimize for **internal team collaboration**. Hackathons require **external-facing workflows** where:
- Participants need a simple, intuitive intake interface
- Organizers need oversight and automation
- Judges need focused evaluation interfaces
- Everyone needs transparency and communication

Generic tools are built for the happy path (the internal team that knows the tool). They're not built for large-scale events where participants and judges are diverse and have different technical skills.

## What Specialized Hackathon Platforms Provide

A purpose-built hackathon platform handles:

1. **Intake**: Participants see a beautiful, clear form. Not a spreadsheet.

2. **Workflow automation**: When a project is submitted, judges automatically get notified. When judging is complete, leaderboards auto-calculate and update.

3. **Role-based interfaces**: Participants see submission forms. Judges see evaluation rubrics. Organizers see dashboards. No confusion.

4. **Structured judging**: Rubrics, blind review, score aggregation, audit trails—built in from day one.

5. **Live event support**: Real-time leaderboards, judge dashboards, announcements. Built for energy and spectacle.

6. **Notification management**: Smart alerts that don't spam everyone. Judges know when assignments are ready. Participants know when decisions are made.

7. **Integration**: Connects with your existing tools (Slack, GitHub, email, etc.) without forcing everyone into a new home for all their data.

## When You Might Use Generic Tools (But Shouldn't)

**The argument:** "We have Airtable/Notion/Jira, so why pay for something else?"

**The answer:** Forcing a hackathon onto a generic tool costs more in:
- Organizer time spent building custom workflows
- Judge frustration (poor experience = bad quality judging)
- Participant confusion (clunky intake = lower submissions)
- Lack of automation (manual reconciliation = errors + hours of work)

A $300–$1,000 specialized platform pays for itself in organizer time (10-20 hours saved) plus better event quality.

## Real Example

**University runs internal hackathon using Airtable:**
- Day 1: Opened Airtable form to submissions. 50 projects submitted.
- Day 2: Shared spreadsheet with judges. All judges can see all projects (no blind review).
- Day 3: Judges email back feedback in different formats from different people. Organizer spends 3 hours reconciling.
- Day 4: Manual calculation of scores. Discover two judges used different scoring scales (1-5 vs 1-10). Need to restart.
- Day 5: Finally announce winners. Process took 40 hours of organizer time.

**Same university using FairArena:**
- Day 1: Registration + submission intake automated. Clean UI. 50 projects submitted.
- Day 2: Blind review automatically activated. Judges assigned by expertise. All on same scoring rubric. Instant audit trail.
- Day 3: Scores auto-calculated. Leaderboard auto-generated. Organizer reviews for anomalies (takes 30 minutes).
- Day 4: Announce winners publicly. Judges assign winners with published reasoning.
- Day 5 onwards: Process took 3 hours of organizer time. Winners + feedback generated automatically.

**Time saved**: 37 hours per hackathon. **Quality improvement**: Transparent, fair judging vs political decisions.

## Recommendation: Hybrid Approach

**Here's what works:**
1. Use **Airtable/Notion** for post-event stuff: archiving projects, building a retrospective database, documenting learnings
2. Use a **specialized hackathon platform** for the actual event: registration → submission → judging → announcement
3. Integrate the specialized platform with **Slack** for team communication
4. Export final results to **Airtable** for your knowledge base

This gives you:
- The structure and automation of a purpose-built tool
- The flexibility of Airtable for historical data
- The simplicity of Slack for team communication
- No lock-in; you own all your data

## Conclusion

Generic tools (Airtable, Notion, Jira) are powerful for their intended purpose. But they're not designed for event management or structured judging workflows.

**Specialization matters.** A hackathon platform is built specifically for:
- Intake at scale
- Fair, transparent judging
- Automation and workflows
- Live event energy
- Diverse participant experiences

Trying to force Airtable or Notion to do this work is like using a hammer to drive screws. Technically possible, painful to execute, and the result is worse than using the right tool.

**Today, try a specialized platform**: See how much friction disappears when the tool is built for exactly what you're doing.
