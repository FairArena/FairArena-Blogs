# Multi-Round Judging: The Structure That Ensures Quality Decisions

Imagine asking 20 judges to evaluate 100 projects in one go.

**What happens:**
- Judges rush through projects (judge fatigue)
- Early judgments influence later ones (anchoring bias)
- Great projects get overlooked if they're unlucky to be #87 (recency bias)
- All 100 projects judged equally, even though only 5 are competitive

**This is inefficient and poor decision-making.**

**Multi-round judging** is better. You screen projects through multiple phases, reducing load and improving quality with each round.

## The Multi-Round Model

### **Round 1: Screening / Triage**
- **Goal**: Quick pass/fail; eliminate obvious non-starters
- **Judge count**: All judges (or a smaller screening committee)
- **Per-project time**: 5-10 minutes
- **Rubric**: Simplified (2-3 criteria: Does it fit our criteria? Is it feasible?)
- **Decision**: Top 20-30% advance

### **Round 2: Deep Evaluation**
- **Goal**: Serious evaluation of competitive projects
- **Judge count**: Specialty judges (match judge expertise to project category)
- **Per-project time**: 30-45 minutes
- **Rubric**: Full rubric (4-6 criteria with detailed scoring)
- **Decision**: Top 5-10 advance to finals

### **Round 3: Deliberation / Finals**
- **Goal**: Leadership discusses finalists; make final selection
- **Judge count**: Small panel (3-5 decision-makers)
- **Per-project time**: 1-2 hours of deep discussion
- **Rubric**: Context-aware (understanding of trade-offs, strategic fit)
- **Decision**: Select winners

## Why Multi-Round Works Better

### **Reduces Judge Fatigue**
- Round 1: Quick screening (each judge ~2 hours total)
- Round 2: Deep evaluation of smaller pool (each judge ~3-4 hours)
- Round 3: Leadership deliberation (select group, ~2-3 hours)
- **Total**: Reasonable time commitment vs. asking everyone to evaluate 100 projects for 8 hours

### **Improves Quality**
- Round 1 eliminates obvious non-starters → judges don't waste time on those
- Round 2 goes deep on competitive projects → serious evaluation
- Round 3 allows deliberation → wisdom of crowd, nuanced understanding

### **Reduces Biases**
- Round 1 uses unanimous screening (eliminates outliers)
- Round 2 uses multiple evaluators per project
- Round 3 uses discussion (flags are discussed openly)

### **Increases Transparency**
- Participants understand the process
- They know projects were evaluated multiple times
- They see clear advancement criteria at each stage

## Real Example: Hackathon with 150 Projects

**Round 1 (Screening, 1 day)**
- All 15 judges receive simple rubric
- Each judge evaluates all 150 projects quickly (5-10 min each)
- Criteria: Does this solve a problem? Is it technically feasible?
- Judges' consensus: Top 40 projects advance (27%)
- Eliminated: 110 projects that aren't competitive

**Round 2 (Deep Dive, 1 week)**
- 15 judges assigned 40 projects (3 judges per project)
- Full rubric: Innovation, feasibility, impact, design, presentation
- Dedicated time (40 projects × 30 min × 3 judges = 60 hours total)
- Judges' verdict: Top 10 projects advance
- Eliminated: 30 projects that had promise but don't compare to finalists

**Round 3 (Finals, 1 day)**
- 5 senior judges review top 10 projects in depth
- Panel discussion: "Why is Project A better than Project B?"
- Public presentation + judging if live event
- Winners announced

**Result**: High-quality decision, reasonable judge time, transparent process.

## Variant: Rolling Scores (Majority Consensus Model)

Instead of strict cutoffs, some competitions use rolling advancement:

- **Round 1**: Projects scored 1-10; all scoring 7+ auto-advance
- **Round 2**: Remaining projects scored 1-10; all scoring 8+ auto-advance
- **Round 3**: Remaining projects discussed; judging room decides top 3

**Advantage**: No arbitrary cutoffs. Advancement based on actual score.

## Variant: Single-Blind Multi-Round

Combine blind review + multi-round:

- **Round 1 (Blind)**: Judges evaluate anonymously; projects screened
- **Round 2 (Blind)**: Smart judges evaluate; projects deep-reviewed
- **Round 3 (Unblind)**: Identities revealed; deliberation with full context
- **Result**: Fair initial evaluation + strategic deliberation with all info

**Why this works**: Early bias is removed; final decision is informed.

## Variant: Bracket-Style Competition

Project elimination tournament style:

- **Round 1**: 64 projects → 32 winners (head-to-head comparison)
- **Round 2**: 32 projects → 16 winners
- **Round 3**: 16 projects → 8 winners
- **Round 4**: 8 → 4  (semi-finals)
- **Round 5**: 4 → 2 (finals)
- **Finals**: Best of best

**Great for**: Live events, spectator engagement, clarity of competition
**Challenge**: Requires head-to-head judging (not all competitions support this)

## Designing Your Multi-Round Process

### Step 1: Know Your Volume
- 10-50 projects? May not need multi-round; single elimination with good rubric is fine
- 50-200 projects? Definitely use multi-round
- 200+ projects? 3+ rounds essential

### Step 2: Define Your Rounds
- **What's the goal of each round?** (screening vs. deep evaluation vs. strategy)
- **How many judges per round?** (all vs. experts)
- **What rubric?** (simple vs. full)
- **Advancement criteria?** (top X%, score threshold, unanimous consensus)

### Step 3: Set Timelines
- Give judges enough time per round, but don't drag it out
- Multi-round over 3-4 weeks works well (not too rushed, not eternal)
- Communicate timeline upfront to judges

### Step 4: Train Judges on the Process
- "Here's Round 1: quick screening, 5-10 min per project"
- "Here's Round 2: deep dive, 30-45 min per project"
- "Here's Round 3: we discuss as a panel"
- Set expectations clearly

### Step 5: Implement in Software
- Use a platform that supports multi-round workflows
- Automate project advancement
- Auto-calculate averages and determine who advances
- Judges see clearly "You're evaluating these 40 projects for the Final Round"

## When Single-Round Judging Is Okay

- **< 20 projects**: Everyone evaluates everyone; single round is fine
- **Simple evaluation**: Pass/fail only (e.g., "meets eligibility")
- **Trusted judges**: Small group who understands the domain deeply
- **Community voting**: Participants vote; not judges + deliberation needed

## Pitfalls to Avoid

### **Pitfall 1: Unclear advancement criteria**
- Don't just say "best projects advance"
- Say "Top 20% of projects, based on average score of 7+, advance to round 2"
- Be specific; be transparent

### **Pitfall 2: Inconsistent evaluation**
- Round 1 judges use different rubrics
- Result: Inconsistent advancement; trust lost
- **Fix**: Same rubric across all judges in a round

### **Pitfall 3: Judge overlaps confuse learning**
- Judge sees a project scored 6/10 by Round 1; doesn't revisit in Round 2
- **Fix**: Round 2 judges are fresh reviewers (unless you want consistency)

### **Pitfall 4: Too many rounds (analysis paralysis)**
- 5+ rounds means endless judging
- You need clear advancement criteria so evaluation doesn't drag
- **Best practice**: 2-3 rounds for most competitions

### **Pitfall 5: Feedback doesn't improve**
- Round 1 is quick screening; judges don't provide feedback
- Participants advance to Round 2 but don't know why or what to improve
- **Fix**: Even in Round 1, collect one sentence of feedback per project

## Reporting & Transparency

After multi-round judging, show participants:
- "Your project advanced Round 1 (qualified score: 7.2/10)"
- "Your project was not selected in Round 2 (average score: 6.8/10)"
- "Feedback from judges: [qualitative comments]"
- "You can reapply next year with refinements based on feedback"

Transparency builds trust, even with losers.

## Conclusion

Single-round judging is fine for small events. For serious competitions (50+ projects), multi-round judging:
- **Reduces fatigue**: Judges do focused, manageable work
- **Improves quality**: Screening eliminates noise; deep dive focused on competitive projects
- **Enables fairness**: Multiple judges per project; fewer biases
- **Increases transparency**: Clear process, clear advancement criteria

**Before your next competition, design your rounds:**
- How many projects will you get?
- When do you need decisions?
- Which judges for which rounds?
- What rubric for each round?

Multi-round judging is the professional standard. It signals to participants and judges: "We take this seriously."
